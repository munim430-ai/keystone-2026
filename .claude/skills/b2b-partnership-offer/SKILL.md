# B2B Partnership Offer & Evaluation

Negotiation is won before the first message: by knowing your own leverage, the
partner's real moat, and the single fear your shared customer is paying to
resolve. This skill turns that into a proposal that opens a relationship without
giving away position.

## The mindset (why the tactics below work)

A weak outreach reads as *"please partner with us."* A strong one reads as *"we
each hold something the other can't easily build — here's how it fits."* The
difference is not politeness; it's whether you've done three pieces of homework
first. Do them before writing a word.

### 1. Name your own strongest card
What can you do that the partner values and can't cheaply replicate? Lead with
it. If you bury it, you negotiate from need instead of strength. Write it as one
sentence you could say out loud.

### 2. Find the partner's real moat — the "why not do it myself?" test
The user can often replicate part of what a partner offers (e.g. registering
with universities directly). So the proposal must locate what the partner
provides that the user **genuinely cannot** build from where they sit —
on-the-ground infrastructure, local licenses, physical presence, a network,
regulatory standing, a captive customer base. If you can't name it, that's the
finding: say so, and let the meeting surface it rather than assuming value.

### 3. Anchor on the customer's core fear
Every intermediary business is really selling the resolution of one fear. Name
it explicitly and show your side already resolves it — that is what makes you a
partner worth good terms, not a reseller. (For study-abroad from Bangladesh,
that fear is **visa security**: will the student actually get the visa? A
pipeline of visa-ready, low-risk students is the most valuable thing you can
bring a Korea-side partner.)

## Probing pricing without being naive

Never ask "how much do you charge?" cold — it signals you're shopping, and it
lets them anchor high. Instead, frame the question around **designing a fair,
durable mutual model**, which invites them to reveal structure as a
collaborator:

- "To structure something fair on both sides, help me understand how WeBring is
  set up commercially — is it a per-student settlement fee, a university-side
  arrangement, a student subscription, or a revenue share? We want the model to
  reward the outcome we both care about."
- Offer a hypothesis to make it cheap for them to correct: "We assumed the
  economics sit mostly on the settlement/service side rather than admissions —
  is that right?" People correct a specific guess far more readily than they
  answer an open question.
- Tie price to value you supply: "Since our students arrive visa-secured and
  low-desertion, we'd expect that materially lowers your risk and cost — we'd
  want the terms to reflect that."

The goal is to learn their model **and** plant that your inbound students are a
discount-worthy asset, in the same breath.

## Surfacing "why you, not me?" without insult

The user may already do part of the partner's job. Say so — respectfully — as a
way to focus the conversation on real synergy, not to belittle:

- "We already handle admissions end-to-end and can register with universities
  directly, so where we see the real synergy isn't admissions — it's [the thing
  only they have]. Could you walk us through what your side provides after
  arrival that we can't replicate from Bangladesh, and how it de-risks the
  student?"

This does two things at once: signals you're not a naive lead who needs hand-
holding (raising your status), and makes them articulate their moat (which tells
you whether the partnership is worth it).

## Structure of the deliverable

Default to a warm, concise message — not a contract. Open a door; don't try to
close everything in writing. Use this shape, adapting length to the channel
(a chat reply is shorter than an emailed proposal):

1. **Warm acknowledgement** — accept the meeting/interest; establish peer footing.
2. **Who we are, in one strong line** — lead with your strongest card (§1).
3. **The customer fear we already resolve** — anchor on it (§3); show your side handles it.
4. **What we're exploring together** — name the specific synergy (their moat, §2), not a vague "partnership."
5. **The smart questions** — 2–4 crisp questions that (a) probe pricing via the fair-model frame and (b) draw out their unique value / the "why you" answer. Phrase them as a collaborator, not an interrogator.
6. **A concrete next step** — propose the call with a couple of time options; keep momentum.

Keep it human. Specific beats clever. Never oversell; a confident, curious tone
outperforms a hard pitch with a partner who approached you.

## Output format & housekeeping

- Produce the message in the language the relationship is being conducted in;
  if unsure, write clear professional English and offer a translation.
- If the user wants a polished document (PDF/branded), write the content first,
  then hand off to the docx/PDF build step they already use in this repo.
- End with a short **"why this works"** note to the user (2–4 bullets) explaining
  the leverage behind each move, so they can adapt it live in the meeting.
- If a fact you'd need (their exact pricing, their license scope) isn't known,
  do not invent it — turn it into one of the smart questions instead.
