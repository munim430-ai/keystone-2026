# Chapter 7: The Embassy Cover Letter (D-2 Visa Application)

## Core Idea
The cover letter is a one-page, 5-section address to the Visa Officer at the Korean Embassy Dhaka that fronts the visa file — identity, background, why Korea, why this university, and who pays.

## Format
- **To:** The Visa Officer, Embassy of the Republic of Korea, 4 Madani Ave, Dhaka-1212
- **Subject (pick one):** "Application for D-2 Student Visa to Study in South Korea" / "Request for Issuance of Student Visa (D-2) for South Korea" (and variants)
- **Salutation:** Dear Sir,
- **Header fields:** Date; sign-off with Respectfully, NAME / PHONE / EMAIL

## The 5 Sections
1. **About yourself (only tell yourself):** official name, passport number, permanent address (village/post office/police station/district), current enrollment; state the request — a student visa to attend [university] and finish the degree in [subject]; why this university is the top pick (academic resources, reputation).
2. **Background details:** academic background, accomplishments, awards, volunteer work — **and cover any year gaps.**
3. **Why this nation:** Korea's beauty, nature, culture, hospitality; similarities between the two nations; Korea's notability in economics and education.
4. **Why this university (with future plan):** scholarships, reasonably priced tuition, facilities, research, professor experience; many students from home country attend ("I can speak with them easily"); the whole future strategy — how the degree benefits you, what knowledge you seek.
5. **Conclusion (with your sponsor):** sponsor's identity and relationship; "he will cover every expense while I am in Korea"; gratitude to the reader; all documentation attached; eagerly await response.

## Decision Rules
- Year gaps go in section 2 of the letter AND in the SOP AND in the gap certificate — one consistent story in three places.
- The sponsor named in section 5 must be the same person as in the financial affidavit and bank documents — name-match discipline (see Gachon's spelling rule, Ch 5).
- Keep it to one page; the letter is a map of the file, not the file.

## Anti-patterns
- **Letter-SOP divergence**: different reasons for choosing Korea in the two documents invites interview probing.
- **Naming no sponsor**: section 5 exists because "who pays?" is the officer's core question — never leave it implicit.

## Key Takeaways
1. Same skeleton every time; only the personal content changes — template it.
2. Sections 1–5 mirror the visa officer's mental checklist: who / credible? / why here? / plan? / funded?
3. It's the first page the officer reads — errors here color the whole file.
